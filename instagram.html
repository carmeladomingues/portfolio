Your Next Event On Instagram

This case study brings a seamless way to discover, share, and plan events, making it personal, interactive, and social.

ROLE

UX/UI Designer in a solo personal project

SCOPE

Discover and research, benchmarking, low-fidelity wireframes, high-fidelity prototypes, UX research, market research, mobile application

TOOLS

Figma and Figjam

TIMEFRAME

3 weeks

Project Overview Why Instagram? Hypothesis Persona Creation Process Ideation Prototype Learnings

Project Overview

A purpose for a career transition

With a background in Graphic Design and Marketing, I had always been drawn to UX/UI. After nearly six years in the field, including two as the lead of Social Media & Design at an IT company, I decided it was time to make the transition official. With the support of our Design Director, I was invited to present a project to the US design team board.

I spent an entire weekend brainstorming ideas for this project until, after missing a party I really wanted to go to, the answer hit me. It got me thinking about the old Facebook Events feature, where you could find local happenings, see who was going, and actually feel connected to what was around you. I missed that. And then I thought: what if Instagram had that?

Why Instagram?

Research

To back up my idea, I did some desk research. One stat stood out: in a 2023 survey with 1,033 Brazilian respondents, 64% already use social networks for online research, with Gen Z leading the shift away from traditional search engines. If people are already living on Instagram, why not find their events there too?

Source: Revista Casa e Jardim — Estudo aponta que frequentar festas e shows aumenta expectativa de vida

Insights

Fragmentation of Information:

Demonstrates how event information is scattered across different sources, making it difficult to find relevant events.

Lack of Personalization:

Illustrates how the lack of personalization in event searches overwhelms users with events that don't align with their interests.

Limitations in Interaction:

An example of how the absence of a dedicated platform for interaction during events results in missed opportunities to make new connections and expand one's social network.

Hypothesis

Proof of concept

From the research, two hypotheses emerged:

Consolidating event information into a single platform would increase community engagement.

Integrating interaction tools into event platforms would help attendees connect and reduce feelings of isolation.

To validate these hypotheses, I interviewed a small group of friends who are heavy Instagram users. Most expressed nostalgia for Facebook's event features and said they find it tedious to search for party accounts just to see what's happening on the weekends. I also used Instagram Stories' Questions feature to gather broader feedback from my followers, asking whether they missed having an events feature on the platform. The responses confirmed there was a real appetite for it.

Persona

Luana Silva, 24 | Graphic Designer | SP, Brazil

Luana is a young, creative professional who works as a graphic designer in a trendy agency. She's a heavy social media user and loves exploring the vibrant cultural scene of her city and is always on the lookout for the latest parties, art exhibitions, and live music events.

Interests

  • Going to parties, music concerts, and art exhibitions.
  • Discovering unique experiences in the city.
  • Engaging with friends.

Goals

  • Discover local events without having to search multiple platforms.
  • Stay connected with friends and the cultural scene in one place.

Pains

  • Fragmented Information: struggles to track events across multiple platforms.
  • Boring Recommendations: gets generic suggestions that don't match her taste.
  • Missed Events: finds out about things too late.

Why the feature would help her?

  • She's already on Instagram, no new app needed.
  • Event recommendations based on who she follows.
  • She can see which friends are going.

To better understand the target users, I created a persona that captured their goals, interests, and pain points, keeping their needs at the center of every design decision.

Creation Process

Goals in mind

With Luana's needs in mind, it was time to start building.

1.

Make it super easy to find all kinds of local events: whether you're into art, music, sports, or just want to party all night long.

4.

Keep everyone in the loop about upcoming events and important updates with personalized notifications, no more missing events!

2.

Personalize the experience so users can filter events like a pro, choosing what they want based on location, event type, and date, because nobody wants to stumble into a boring lecture on a Saturday night!

5.

Make planning social activities by allowing users to save events, share them with friends, and maybe even coordinate who brings the snacks!

3.

Boost the social vibes by letting users RSVP to events, chat with fellow mates, and share their epic experiences, let's make some memories!

6.

Create an Instagram feature that's as easy to use ensuring users have a fun time navigating and interacting without getting lost in confusion!

Benchmarking

Before diving into design, I played detective and looked at what competitors were already doing, especially since most users I interviewed mentioned Facebook with nostalgia.

I analyzed the mobile version of four competitors to gather insights and shape the feature.

Ingresse 🇧🇷

✅

The feature has a clean aesthetic that opens directly to the user's current location, showcasing upcoming events right away.

It has good usability, making it easy for users to navigate and find what they're looking for.

❌

However, one downside is that the date and time appear too small on the screen, which needs to be improved.

Sympla 🇧🇷

✅

The feature includes a great search bar that allows users to easily find what they're looking for. Events are categorized, making it simple to navigate.

It highlights the most viewed events from the last 24 hours, which adds a sense of urgency and excitement.

❌

While it works well overall, it feels a bit crowded, and could use some simplification for a better experience.

Baladapp 🇧🇷

❌

It doesn't open directly to the user's location, requiring them to enter that information manually.

Additionally, the highlighted events take up a lot of screen space, creating a cluttered appearance that detracts from the overall user experience.

Facebook 🇧🇺🇸

✅

The feature includes a "Create Event" button, which is a great addition I hadn't considered before. It also has well defined categories, making it easy for users to navigate through different types of events.

Additionally, it shows events based on the GPS location from the user's phone, ensuring that users can discover activities happening nearby.

Userflow

Before jumping into wireframes, I mapped out the user flow to make sure every possible move had a clear path, for me and for the user.

Ideation

Low fidelity

This was my first project in Figma. I had taken a beginner's course, but I learn best by doing, so I jumped in. My Photoshop background helped with the basics, and the rest was trial and error.

Before even opening Figma, I sketched every wireframe on paper and tested it with 5 Instagram users to get early feedback.

Mid fidelity

With the sketches validated, I moved to Figma and built out the wireframes for both user flows, which made the jump to UI design a lot smoother.

Prototype

High fidelity

Learnings

What this project taught me

This project taught me more than I expected. Reading books like The Design of Everyday Things pulled me deeper into design theory, but the real learning happened by doing. I built everything from scratch, figured things out through trial and error, and discovered that empathy isn't just a buzzword, it's what makes a design actually work. Every conversation with friends and mentors added something new.

And then there was Figma. I went from knowing almost nothing to building every component from scratch (including figuring out what a component even was). Auto-layout felt like a nightmare, but I made it through.

Even though the job position ended up being canceled, the feedback and lessons from those three weeks only made me more certain: I want a long career in UX/UI.
