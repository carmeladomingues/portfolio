Project Overview

A purpose for a career transition

With a background in Graphic Design and Marketing, I had always been drawn to UX/UI. After nearly six years in the field, including two as the lead of Social Media & Design at an IT company, I decided it was time to make the transition official. With the support of our Design Director, I was invited to present a project to the US design team board.

I spent an entire weekend brainstorming ideas for this project until, after missing a party I really wanted to go to, the answer hit me. It got me thinking about the old Facebook Events feature, where you could find local happenings, see who was going, and actually feel connected to what was around you. I missed that. And then I thought: what if Instagram had that?

Why Instagram?

Research

Instagram has over 2 billion monthly active users, with 70% of them under 34 years old. It's where people go to discover, be inspired, and stay connected — but events? That's still a gap. Despite being a platform built on visual storytelling, Instagram has no native way to discover, share, or plan events together.

Proof of concept

Instagram tested an Events tab in 2021 but quietly removed it. The demand was there — the execution wasn't refined enough. This gave me confidence that the problem was real, and the opportunity was still open.

What existed

Facebook Events — a feature that allowed users to discover and RSVP to local events, see who's going, and share with friends.

The gap

Instagram, despite its visual nature and massive user base, had no equivalent feature — leaving event discovery fragmented across Stories, bios, and DMs.

Hypothesis

If Instagram had a native events feature...

Users would be more likely to discover, attend, and share local events — increasing engagement and time spent on the platform, while helping businesses and creators reach relevant audiences more effectively.

The hypothesis was grounded in three pillars: discoverability, social proof, and seamless planning — all things Instagram already does well in other contexts.

Creation Process

Goals in mind

Before jumping into design, I mapped out the core goals the feature needed to achieve — from the user's perspective and from Instagram's business perspective.

01

Make it easy to discover events that feel personally relevant

02

Add social context — see who else is going, invite friends

03

Keep it native to Instagram — no new app, no friction

04

Help creators and businesses reach the right audience

05

Make planning and RSVPing feel effortless and fun

06

Stay consistent with Instagram's existing design language

Benchmarking

I analyzed existing event-related features across major platforms to understand what worked, what didn't, and where the whitespace was.

Facebook

Facebook Events

The gold standard for event discovery. Strong on social proof and RSVP, but the platform has lost younger audiences. The feature exists but the context is wrong.

Eventbrite

Eventbrite

Great for ticketed events and discovery, but lacks the social layer. You don't know who's going unless they're also on Eventbrite and connected to you.

Shotgun

Shotgun

Popular in Europe for nightlife events. Strong visual identity, but limited to a niche audience and requires yet another app download.

Learnings

What this project taught me

Constraints are creative fuel. Working within Instagram's existing design system forced me to be more intentional about every decision.

Research shapes everything. The proof of concept and market data didn't just validate the idea — they guided every design decision.

Social context is underrated. The "who else is going" layer turned out to be the most valuable differentiator from existing solutions.

Native feels better. Integrating the feature into existing navigation rather than creating a new tab made it feel like it always belonged there.
