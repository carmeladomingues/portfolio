Designing for Early Recognition and Support in Domestic Violence

A master's thesis exploring how service design and AI can reach women experiencing domestic violence before they identify themselves as victims, not after.

Project Overview

Recognition doesn't happen in a single moment

Most support systems for domestic violence are built for women who have already named their situation as abusive. This thesis starts earlier, while she's still confused, still doubting, still telling herself it's probably nothing. Using a service design approach, it develops a phase-based framework (P0 to P4) mapping how abusive dynamics unfold over time, then translates it into a consent-based system proposal, embedded in the platforms women already use, and tests it with real participants through mid- and high-fidelity prototypes.

This is not a diagnostic tool and it doesn't replace existing services like APAV or Linha 144. It's an early-stage layer of interpretation, designed to lead women toward the support that already exists, at the moment they're ready for it rather than the moment a system decides they should be.

The Problem

Support exists. It just starts too late.

Domestic violence rarely starts as a single identifiable act. It builds gradually, disguised as care or jealousy, and recognising it is a slow, non-linear process shaped by attachment, self-blame, and fear of judgement. Existing support, from helplines to shelters to campaigns, is almost entirely built for women who already know what's happening and are ready to act. Those still questioning, still explaining it away, are rarely reached by anything.

The research question driving this thesis: how can support systems be designed to reach women experiencing domestic violence before they identify as victims?

The Framework

Mapping abuse across five phases, not one moment

To understand how recognition actually unfolds, this research developed a phase-based framework (P0 to P4), mapping eleven types of abuse across five experiential phases. Movement isn't linear, someone can stay in one phase for years, move back and forth, or experience more than one at once.

P0

Early warning / normalisation

Subtle behaviours reframed as care or love. Easy to miss, easy to explain away.

P1

Pattern building / coercive control onset

Behaviours repeat and tighten into a pattern, still without a name.

P2

Acute episodes / escalation

Explicit incidents that are harder to minimise or deny.

P3

Separation and post-separation abuse

Risk continues, and sometimes intensifies, after leaving.

P4

Recovery and long-term impact

Rebuilding autonomy, trust, and a sense of self over time.

The key finding here: P0 and P1 are defined less by explicit acts of violence and more by an internal process of interpretation. Women in these phases aren't in denial, they're doing exactly what anyone would do with ambiguous, emotionally loaded information: trying to make it make sense.

The Process

From gap to person

The benchmarking showed existing support mostly targets women who already identify as victims, women in P0 and P1 get comparatively little help with recognition. This chapter asks who these women are, translating that structural gap into a human-centred case for the design proposal that follows.

User Research & Abuse Typology

Three abuse typologies became the design lens that turns the benchmarking's structural gaps into concrete profiles: emotional, technology-facilitated, and financial abuse. All three appear early in abusive dynamics and are harder to detect than physical violence, which was deliberately left out, non-physical abuse is consistently the hardest to identify and name early on.

Emotional abuse

Among the most common forms of violence against women in intimate partnerships across Europe. Often subtle and slow to build.[1]

Technology-facilitated abuse

Flagged as an emerging concern across Europe, particularly for younger women. A 2024 Portuguese study found a 72% victimisation rate for tech-facilitated sexual violence.[2]

Financial abuse

An overlooked dimension of domestic violence. Particularly relevant in long-term relationships, where control becomes normalised.[1]

Her profile offers a particularly useful lens, the same digital platforms that may enable the abuse may also enable recognition and help-seeking, if designed with care.

Sofia Cardoso, 22

Master's student · Porto · Tech abuse · P0/P1

"He asked me to share my location so he'd always know I was safe. I thought it was sweet. Now he messages me every time I'm somewhere he didn't expect."

Bio

Sofia is a second-year design student who is active on social media and considers herself tech-savvy. She met Diogo at a university event eight months ago and describes him as attentive and caring. He asked her to share her location so he would always know she was safe and she thought it was sweet. Now he messages her every time she is somewhere he did not expect, checks her phone regularly, and gets upset when she posts photos without telling him first. Sofia has started filtering what she shares online and with friends, telling herself this is what love looks like.

In a relationship for 8 months
Lives with partner No

Phase

Needs

Information about what healthy digital boundaries look like, a private and discreet space to question what she is experiencing, reassurance that wanting privacy is not a sign of dishonesty, and recognition that tech control is a form of abuse, not care.

Goals

Feel free to use her phone and social media without anxiety, understand whether her partner's behaviour is normal, reconnect with friends without feeling monitored, and make sense of her situation without worrying her parents.

Frustrations

Tech abuse is rarely represented in domestic violence resources. She normalises digital control because "everyone shares passwords" and feels guilty for questioning someone who "just wants to be close." Her friends think Diogo is caring and would not understand.

Pain Points

She is aware her partner may see her searches. Existing apps require download, leaving a visible trace. Support platforms feel designed for older women in crisis, and the line between care and control feels genuinely blurry to her.

What the three personas share matters more than what sets them apart: the real challenge isn't the presence of abuse, it's the transition from uncertainty to awareness, and from awareness to action. None of them is actively seeking support, even while their wellbeing is visibly affected.

Comparing these tools against the traditional services from the earlier benchmark shows real gains in access, and the same structural blind spot around interpretation.

What AI improves

  • Available continuously, without the geographic or time constraints of traditional services
  • Embedded in everyday platforms, reducing visibility and risk on a shared or monitored device
  • Tools like Sophia minimise or eliminate digital traces entirely

What AI still misses

  • Almost every tool assumes the user already recognises her situation as abusive
  • Even early-stage tools like Aimee and AinoAid offer generic support, not interpretation
  • None meaningfully adapt language or pathway to the specific form of abuse described

Put together, the AI tools repeat the same pattern found in the traditional benchmark, in a newer format:

Gap 1, early-stage recognition failure. Almost every service analysed assumes the user already knows she's experiencing abuse. Only two of the seven AI tools reviewed, Aimee and AinoAid, explicitly target P0, the phase where intervention has the greatest potential to prevent escalation.

Gap 2, lack of personalised interpretation. Even the tools that engage early-stage users respond generically. None differentiate between forms of abuse, despite each requiring a different vocabulary and entry point for recognition.

Gap 3, a fragmented ecosystem. No tool follows a woman continuously across the full P0–P4 journey. Every intervention is a disconnected, one-off touchpoint, and only one of the seven tools (Sophia) even allows her to save or document what she's experiencing.

These three gaps became the brief for the design proposal.

Design Drivers & Criteria

Four recurring gaps became the drivers behind the proposal, each paired with the concrete rule it translates into.

01

Early intervention → No forced disclosure

Support clusters around crisis and recovery, almost nothing exists for P0/P1 doubt. She tells her story in her own words, no account or formal disclosure required.

02

Emotional interpretation → Reflection before referral

Access to information isn't the barrier, making sense of it is. Crisis resources stay available but aren't pushed on first contact.

03

Continuity → Pause and return

Every existing service is a disconnected, one-off touchpoint. She can leave for weeks and come back in context.

04

Personalisation → Type-aware language

Most tools respond generically regardless of abuse type. Vocabulary and examples adapt to what she actually describes.

The system does not wait for a woman to call herself a victim.

It creates a private, gradual path from uncertainty to understanding, and from understanding to support.

Chapter 01 · The proposal

Design Proposal

From gap to intervention

The benchmarking and the AI tools analysis point to the same problem: the moment of greatest need, when a woman is still uncertain whether what she's experiencing is abuse, is the least supported point across the entire landscape. Three gaps drive that: early-stage recognition failure (P0–P1), lack of personalised interpretation, and no continuity across phases.

Recent research shows the pieces already exist separately, language models that detect intimate partner violence patterns in social media discourse[1], that classify survivors' information needs from online posts[2], and trauma-informed models that identify emotional register and help-seeking intent[3]. What doesn't yet exist is a system that applies this capacity before formal help-seeking begins, consent-based rather than built for detection. That's the contribution this proposal offers.

[1]Al-Garadi, M. A., Kim, S., Guo, Y., Warren, E., Yang, Y.-C., Lakamana, S., & Sarker, A. (2022). Natural language model for automatic identification of intimate partner violence reports from Twitter. Array, 15, 100217.

[2]Guan, S., Hui, V., Stiglic, G., Constantino, R. E., Lee, Y. J., & Wong, A. K. C. (2025). Classifying the information needs of survivors of domestic violence in online health communities using large language models. Journal of Medical Internet Research, 27, e65397.

[3]Research conducted at Virginia Tech (2025), trauma-informed AI model for emotional register and help-seeking intent.

System Overview

The proposal is a phase-aware support system embedded in platforms women already use, Google, TikTok, ChatGPT, Reddit, not a dedicated app to find and download. Built on four principles:

01

Embedded support

No new platform to seek out. The system meets her inside the environments she's already in, reducing risk on shared or monitored devices.

02

Consent-based interaction

No passive tracking, no hidden intervention. Every step, from a first response to a private note, is initiated by her.

03

Early-stage interpretation

Built for someone who hasn't named her experience yet, not for someone already searching for help.

04

Continuity across P0 to P4

She can pause for weeks and come back without losing context, instead of restarting with a new, disconnected tool each time.

These principles are carried by a five-layer architecture, from an invisible platform integration and phase estimation layer, to the conversational layer she actually experiences, to a progressive support layer that only introduces documentation tools and institutional resources like APAV, Linha 144, and RNAVVD once she's ready. Each layer has its own input, output, and moment where it matters most, pick one to see how it actually works.

Ethical boundaries, across all five layers: the system never diagnoses, confirms a detection, or labels the user as a victim, and it doesn't run any surveillance logic beyond what's needed to adapt its response, no identifiable data is retained once a session ends. These are a condition of the design, not an optional safeguard.

Design Decisions

Two decisions worth flagging beyond the principles above.

AI ethics & model choice

No purpose-built model trained on survivor data, that raises consent and bias concerns. Existing LLMs configured through prompt engineering and a curated knowledge base (RAG), transparent and auditable.

Phase-based adaptation

The P0–P4 framework isn't just descriptive, it's the mechanism that changes system behaviour. In P0 it reflects without naming abuse. In P1 it gently introduces documentation, only once patterns recur.

Chapter 02 · Validation

Prototype & Validation

Testing the idea with real people

The prototype wasn't tested for technical performance. It was tested against five objectives: could participants understand the concept without it being explained, did they trust the privacy of the private logging, did the tone feel credible, did the specific features feel useful, and would they actually use or recommend it.

8 participants, ages 26–32 (M = 29), Brazil · Portugal · France · Germany
6/8 completed the post-session questionnaire for quantitative analysis

Remote think-aloud sessions, 31 May – 3 June 2026. Each participant explored the mid-fidelity prototype via Figma against a background scenario describing a confusing relationship, without ever being told the study was about domestic violence in advance.

Findings: Quantitative

4.50 relevance to a real-life situation, out of 5
4.33 overall experience, ease of understanding, likelihood to use
4.00 comfort with the system's pattern-detection approach
6/6 valued not needing to download an app, and support within platforms already in use

Asked where they'd actually seek help, participants most often named a therapist (5/6), then Google, an AI assistant, TikTok, and friends or family (4/6 each), reinforcing the case for meeting them inside platforms they already use. Only two concerns came up: data collection and AI pattern-detection, one participant each.

Findings: Qualitative

Thematic analysis across all eight sessions surfaced five recurring themes.

＋

Non-intrusive presence. Participants wanted intervention only after a pattern recurs, not immediately, gradual felt more appropriate than direct warnings.

＋

Conversational tone. Google read as more impersonal; TikTok and Reddit felt more relatable, reinforcing the case for platform-specific tone rather than one script everywhere.

＋

Privacy. The most raised concern overall, tied to shared devices and partner surveillance. The Face ID-protected shortcut was seen as valuable.

＋

Progressive support. Participants agreed introducing institutional resources too early risks disengagement. The AI's role read as a guide, not a diagnostic tool.

＋

Opportunities for refinement. Clearer terminology, more visible controls, and sharper differentiation between platform pathways.

Support needs to be embedded into spaces people already use, delivered incrementally, and built around autonomy and privacy. These findings shaped the high-fidelity prototype below.

Chapter 03 · Reflection

Limitations & Reflection

What this doesn't answer yet

The system was evaluated through a prototype, not a working product, so feedback reflects perceptions of the concept rather than sustained real-world engagement, on a small, exploratory sample. Technical implementation remains open too: language models can already detect patterns in survivor discourse, but governance, transparency, and accountability still need real answers before this could responsibly go live.

Participants also pointed past the four platforms tested here, toward everyday services like food delivery or banking apps, environments that could reach women who aren't actively searching for help at all. That, and real partnerships with domestic violence organisations, is where this would need to go next.

What this project left me with

This thesis pushed me to design for a moment most systems overlook, the one before someone has language for what's happening to them. Good design doesn't wait for people to arrive fully formed with a clear problem, it meets them earlier, in the mess of figuring things out.

Beyond the framework and the prototype, it changed how I think about care as a design material, not a tone applied at the end, but a structural decision made at every layer.

A few key lessons stayed:

Awareness is a process, not a moment. Designing for gradual recognition instead of a single "aha" changed how I thought about onboarding, tone, and pacing.

Consent has to be structural. It can't be a toggle. It has to shape every layer of the system, from architecture down to a single line of copy.

Sensitive research demands restraint. Every interview, every prototype decision, every word choice had to be weighed against the risk of causing harm rather than support.

A concept is only as strong as its edges. Deciding what the system explicitly isn't, a diagnosis, a substitute for APAV, mattered as much as defining what it is.
